Notes & methodology — performance bands, sunset goals, median vs. total, projections
Important caveat on these bands: Not all low scores represent a failure of execution. A goal scoring 1 or 2 points because of engineering dependencies, stakeholder unavailability, or organisational blockers outside the design team's control tells a very different story from a goal that simply wasn't progressed. Where scores sit in the Failure or Acceptable bands, it is worth distinguishing between execution failures (the team did not drive the work) and external failures (the team was blocked by factors they could not control). Goal 6 (Swift) in March and Goal 16 (Collab Lab) are examples of the latter. This context should always accompany the numbers when presenting to senior stakeholders.

Goal 8 — Roadmap Visibility was sunset at the end of April 2026 following a strategic decision to cease pursuing it due to a lack of organisational traction. Its historical scores (Jan–Apr) remain in the record and count toward totals for those months. From May onward, Goal 8 is excluded from the max possible calculation, median, and projections. The year-end maximum therefore reflects 12 active goals rather than 13. This is a deliberate strategic decision, not a performance failure, and should be framed as such when presenting to senior stakeholders.

What the gold line means in each view — they are not the same measurement:

In the Individual goals view, the gold line is the median: at each month, it finds the goal sitting in the middle of the pack (on that goal's own 0–48 points-for-the-year scale) and plots its value. It answers "is a typical goal in good shape?" Because it's a median, it deliberately ignores how badly the worst-performing goals are doing — a goal stuck at 1 point doesn't drag the median down unless enough other goals are also struggling, since the median only tracks whoever happens to be in the middle. This makes it a more honest single-line summary of overall delivery health than an average would be: with a small team running a spread of goals at very different stages (some just started, some sunset, some blocked by factors outside design's control), a couple of extreme scores at either end could otherwise swing a single average wildly from month to month, whereas the median stays anchored to what's actually typical.
See a worked example with this month's actual numbers


In the Total accumulated view, the gold line changes to a completely different calculation: the team total, which sums every active goal's actual score and compares it against the combined max-possible across all of them. It answers "of everything that was theoretically achievable, how much did we actually capture?" Unlike the median, every single goal counts here — so a small number of consistently weak or stalled goals (e.g. Goal 9, Goal 16) directly pull this figure down, even though they barely move the median in the other view.

This is why the two views can land in different performance bands at the same point in time: the median is protected from outliers by design, while the total has no such protection. Treat them as answering different questions rather than expecting them to agree.
